The reason your application is using so much GPU VRAM at a batch size of 256 is due to the dimensionality of your `optimizationResults` buffer.

Right now, your kernel's `@Parallel` loop is flattened across **Batch × Stocks × Grid**.
If you process `256 candidates` × `8,000 stocks` × `27 grid variations` × `5 output floats` × `4 bytes`, the result buffer alone consumes over **1.1 GB of contiguous VRAM**. Furthermore, OpenCL restricts single buffer allocations (usually capping them at 25% or 50% of your total VRAM), which is why it often fails on Colab.

You can drastically reduce memory usage **by up to 95% without losing a single decimal of precision**. We can achieve this by doing a **partial reduction directly inside the GPU**. By parallelizing only across `Batch × Stocks` and running the `Grid` calculations in a standard `for` loop inside the thread, the VRAM footprint of your results drops from ~1.1 GB down to **~40 MB**.

Here are the exact 3 modifications to make in `TornadoVmOptimizer.java`:

Move the `gridCount` loop inside the thread. This collapses the output dimensions significantly while maintaining enough threads (over 2 million) to keep the GPU fully saturated and running at peak speed.

### Why this is better:

1. **Solves the Memory Issue instantly:** You bypass OpenCL single-buffer limitations entirely.
2. **Speed increase:** While the kernel executes sequentially over `gridCount`, we've massively reduced global memory bandwidth (`VRAM Write` operations). Given GPU latency is usually bound by memory traffic rather than FLOPs, writing 40 MB back to the host is significantly faster than writing 1.1 GB.
3. **No Precision Loss:** We are simply reordering the commutative floating-point additions. The numerical results will be identical.
